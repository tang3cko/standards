# Masahiro Sakurai's Game Design Philosophy

A comprehensive reference of design principles from Masahiro Sakurai (creator of Kirby and Super Smash Bros.), primarily sourced from his YouTube channel "Masahiro Sakurai on Creating Games" (2022-2024, 260 episodes) and his GDC talks, Famitsu columns, and interviews.

---

## 1. Risk and Reward (Game Essence)

### Principle

The fundamental essence of games lies in the relationship between risk and reward, and the resulting push-and-pull dynamic. Players take risks to earn rewards, and this tension is what makes gameplay inherently engaging.

> "Game essence is 'risk and return,' and the resulting 'push and pull.' In other words, taking risks to gain returns is the essence of games."

### Explanation

Sakurai considers risk-and-reward to be the core framework that underpins virtually all game design. When players willingly accept danger or difficulty in exchange for potential benefits, the resulting tension creates engagement. The key is that players must actively choose to take on risk, creating a decision-making loop that drives gameplay forward.

He acknowledges that some genres (rhythm games, visual novels, simulation games) do not primarily rely on this element, but for action and competitive games, it is foundational.

### Application Examples

- **Space Invaders**: Moving closer to enemies increases risk but also makes them easier to hit (reward). The descending aliens gradually raise tension.
- **Super Mario Bros.**: Running faster covers more ground (reward) but increases risk of falling into pits or hitting enemies.
- **Kid Icarus: Uprising - Fiend's Cauldron**: Players spend in-game currency (Hearts) to raise difficulty on a scale with 88 levels of granularity (0.0 to 9.0). Higher difficulty yields better weapon drops and more Hearts, but failure costs the bet and lowers difficulty. This is risk-and-reward made into an explicit, player-controlled mechanic.
- **Super Smash Bros. - Shoryuken Command**: The input difficulty of executing the Shoryuken command represents risk; landing the powerful move is the reward. Sakurai breaks down how the input method itself contains inherent risk-and-reward.

### Review Checkpoints

- Does the game present meaningful risks that players voluntarily accept?
- Are rewards proportional to the risks taken?
- Is there a clear push-and-pull dynamic that creates tension?
- Can the player feel the tension escalate and release through gameplay?
- Are there moments where the player must weigh risk vs. safety?

## 2. Squeeze and Release (Tension and Catharsis)

### Principle

Games must stress the player and then provide a means to release that stress. The method of building tension should directly correspond to how the player can relieve it. This cycle of tension and catharsis is fundamental to a satisfying gameplay experience.

> "The way in which a game stresses out a player should also be the way in which they can remove the tension by clearing the obstacle."

### Explanation

Every game must stress the player at least somewhat, because without challenge there is no fun. However, the critical design insight is that the buildup of tension and its resolution should be structurally connected. When a player overcomes the source of tension, the catharsis is proportional to the difficulty endured. Poorly designed games either never release tension (frustrating) or never build it (boring).

### Application Examples

- **Super Smash Bros.**: Damage accumulates (tension), making players increasingly vulnerable, until a powerful finishing blow sends the opponent flying (catharsis). The percentage display constantly reminds players of mounting danger.
- **Kirby's Dream Land**: Boss encounters compress tension into focused encounters, but Kirby's forgiving health system ensures the release is achievable for players of all skill levels.
- **Kid Icarus: Uprising**: Chapters alternate between aerial rail-shooting (high-action tension) and ground-based exploration (varied pacing), creating a natural rhythm of squeeze and release.

### Review Checkpoints

- Does the game build tension intentionally before releasing it?
- Is the release mechanism directly connected to the source of tension?
- Is the catharsis proportional to the difficulty of the challenge?
- Does the game avoid long stretches of either constant tension or complete relaxation?
- Can the player feel a satisfying emotional arc within each gameplay segment?

## 3. Player Perspective First

### Principle

Game design must always prioritize the player's perspective over the developer's convenience or personal vision. The designer's job is to see the game through the player's eyes and ensure the experience serves the player.

> "See things from the player's perspective. Don't limit the possibilities of gameplay."

> "I focus entirely on the consumer. In fact, there are times where I make games in genres that I'm not very good at."

### Explanation

Sakurai has consistently put the player's experience at the center of every design decision throughout his career. He goes to extreme lengths to understand player expectations, including reportedly disguising himself as a part-time game shop employee to observe consumer behavior. Team members may prioritize ease for game creators rather than fun for players; Sakurai acknowledges this is understandable but warns it should be avoided.

This principle also means not limiting gameplay possibilities. Rather than constraining what players can do, designers should enable diverse play styles and let players discover their own ways to enjoy the game.

### Application Examples

- **Super Smash Bros. Ultimate - "Everyone Is Here"**: Sakurai chose to include every single character from the series' history (86+ fighters) rather than cutting any, because he prioritized player feelings and expectations over development convenience.
- **Kirby's Dream Land**: Designed after Sakurai asked himself what it would take for someone like his mother, who had never played games, to enjoy one.
- **Super Smash Bros. series**: Multiple battle modes, stage options, difficulty settings, items, and music options exist because different players want different experiences from the same game.

### Review Checkpoints

- Has the design been evaluated from the player's perspective, not just the developer's?
- Are design decisions driven by player enjoyment rather than developer convenience?
- Does the game avoid unnecessarily limiting what players can do?
- Are there multiple ways for different types of players to enjoy the game?
- Would someone unfamiliar with the genre understand the appeal?

## 4. The First Three Minutes Rule

### Principle

Players should understand why a game is fun within the first three minutes of playing. Games must communicate their core appeal immediately rather than gating it behind lengthy introductions.

> "Players should understand why a game is fun in the first three minutes."

### Explanation

Drawing from arcade game design wisdom, Sakurai argues that players make rapid judgments about whether a game is worth their time. If developers make players wait too long before experiencing the core gameplay, many will simply put the game down. A slow start is starting on the wrong foot. Developers should not leave players wondering what the actual game is like. Get to the point and jump into gameplay early.

For genres like RPGs where full mechanic introduction is impossible in minutes, Sakurai recommends starting from an exciting point in the story or with a different set of characters (citing Persona 5 and Xenoblade Chronicles as examples of games that use this approach).

### Application Examples

- **Kirby's Dream Land**: The player can immediately move, inhale enemies, and fly. The core appeal is evident in seconds.
- **Super Smash Bros.**: Players are thrown directly into combat. The first match communicates the entire gameplay loop.
- **Persona 5**: Opens with a dramatic heist sequence rather than starting from the chronological beginning, immediately showing the player what kind of experience awaits.

### Review Checkpoints

- Can the player experience the core gameplay loop within the first three minutes?
- Does the opening communicate the game's central appeal?
- Are tutorials integrated into gameplay rather than presented as separate, passive sequences?
- For narrative-heavy games, does the opening start with an exciting hook?
- Is the player actively playing (not just watching) in the first moments?

## 5. Kirbyism: Accessible Depth

### Principle

Games should be easy to pick up but difficult to master. Simplistic controls and low barriers to entry allow beginners to enjoy the game, while deep mechanics and hidden complexity reward experienced players. This philosophy, called "Kirbyism," holds that all games should serve audiences of every skill level.

> "We must aim to satisfy both beginners and experts."

> "If we were to lean towards one kind of player or the other... game development would be easier, but forgoing the pros, or forgoing the beginners, wouldn't result in Smash as it is now, and that's something I hold dear and important."

### Explanation

Sakurai wanted to create games that anyone could enjoy, from complete novices to hardcore competitive players. The key technique is assigning multiple functions to minimal inputs (reducing learning burden) while embedding deep systems beneath the surface. Important game elements ensure that experienced players will find depth, even in a game designed for beginners. Players should be able to choose their own individualized experience.

### Application Examples

- **Kirby's Dream Land**: Playable with just the D-Pad and B button. Press B to inhale/spit, Down to swallow. Yet the Copy ability system in later Kirby games allows experienced players to choose abilities and employ advanced techniques.
- **Super Smash Bros.**: While most fighting games require memorizing complex button combinations, Sakurai simplified inputs to A (normal attack), B (special attack), R (grab), Z (shield), combined with directional inputs. Yet the game supports an incredibly high skill ceiling with advanced techniques like wavedashing, short-hopping, and frame-perfect combos.
- **Kid Icarus: Uprising - Fiend's Cauldron**: 88 degrees of difficulty granularity. Casual players can lower difficulty to 0.0; experts can push to 9.0. The system never excludes either group.
- **Super Smash Bros.**: Computer opponents range from skill level 1 to 9; Classic mode has 5 difficulty levels, allowing fine-tuning to match any player's ability.

### Review Checkpoints

- Can a complete beginner understand the basic controls within minutes?
- Is there hidden depth for experienced players to discover?
- Are controls mapped to minimize the number of inputs needed for basic play?
- Does the difficulty system accommodate a wide range of skill levels?
- Can players choose their own level of engagement without being forced into a single path?
- Does simplification of inputs sacrifice meaningful choice?

## 6. Peaks and Valleys (Character/Element Balance)

### Principle

When balancing game elements (characters, weapons, abilities), preserve and even amplify unique strengths and weaknesses rather than flattening everything toward average. Variety and distinctiveness are the lifeblood of a game.

> "The more similar you make your fighter's abilities, the more they'll start to feel like the same character."

> "If you balance purely based on fighter strength, their abilities quickly start to level out. Shortcomings are balanced while advantages are reduced, leaving you with something average. That doesn't make for a fun game, though."

> "Variety among things like fighters is the very lifeblood of the game. It's what keeps things fresh."

### Explanation

Sakurai's approach to balance rejects the common instinct to nerf strengths and buff weaknesses until all options are equivalent. Instead, he maintains that it is better not to eliminate characters' strong points, but to add other weaknesses to compensate. Each element should have a distinct identity. Players should choose an option because it resonates with their playstyle, not because it has slightly better average stats. There is no charm in something with no strengths or weaknesses.

### Application Examples

- **Super Smash Bros. Ultimate**: All 86+ fighters have nearly identical overall win rates, yet each feels completely different to play. Marth's sword tip does more damage with longer hitstop, while the flat of the blade is weaker, creating a defined risk-reward identity for the character.
- **Super Smash Bros. - Character Selection Criteria**: Characters must display personality in their game, do something only that character can do, consider development and balance feasibility, and maintain franchise representation diversity.
- **Kirby Copy Abilities**: Each Copy ability offers a distinct playstyle. Some are powerful but situational; others are versatile but weak. The player chooses based on preference, not optimal DPS.

### Review Checkpoints

- Do all playable options feel meaningfully distinct from one another?
- Are unique strengths preserved rather than averaged out during balancing?
- Are weaknesses used to offset strengths rather than removing the strengths?
- Does each option offer a reason for a specific type of player to choose it?
- Would a new player be able to identify what makes each option special?

## 7. Stop for Big Moments (Hitstop and Impact Feedback)

### Principle

Momentarily freezing the game during impactful events amplifies the player's sense of power and satisfaction. Hitstop, screen shake, and visual-audio feedback synchronization are essential techniques for making actions feel meaningful.

### Explanation

Sakurai's first "lesson" video on his YouTube channel was titled "Stop for Big Moments," covering the technique of hitstop (hitlag). When a strike connects, both attacker and target freeze for a brief moment, emphasizing impact. The duration of the freeze correlates with the power of the attack. Combined with screen shake, visual effects, and sound design, these micro-pauses transform mechanical inputs into visceral experiences.

In Smash Bros., hitstop duration is determined not only by damage but by attack-specific modifiers. For example, the tip of Marth's sword triggers longer hitstop than the edge of the blade, reinforcing the spatial precision that defines the character's identity.

### Application Examples

- **Super Smash Bros.**: Every attack has a hitstop period. Stronger attacks freeze longer. Critical hits add extra freeze frames and dramatic zoom.
- **Kirby**: Boss defeats often feature slow-motion or freeze effects to punctuate victory.
- **General Principle**: Sakurai demonstrated how classic shoot-em-ups were dramatically improved by adding various implementations of hitstop, making the action clearer and more impactful.

### Review Checkpoints

- Do impactful actions have appropriate hitstop or freeze-frame effects?
- Is the duration of the freeze proportional to the significance of the event?
- Are screen shake and visual effects synchronized with the hitstop?
- Does the feedback make the player feel the weight and consequence of their actions?
- Are these effects used selectively (not for every action, which would dilute impact)?

## 8. Audio as the Soul of the Game

### Principle

No masterpiece has bad audio. Sound design, music, and voice work are not secondary to visuals; they are what hit the player right in the heart. Audio elevates a good game into a great one.

> "No masterpiece has bad audio."

> "Audio is what hits you right in the heart."

> "A game can have bad graphics and still be amazing, but all masterpieces have this one common trait [great audio]."

### Explanation

Sakurai argues that audio design includes not just music composition but sound effects, voice acting, ambient noise, and the strategic delivery of all audio elements. As graphics improved, sound design had to match, with particular attention to ambient noise and how players want to hear the game world around them. Music tempo must match the pace and progression of gameplay. Each track must be identifiable yet have its own appeal. Composers may not have a full picture of game progression, so directors must ensure alignment between audio and gameplay pacing.

### Application Examples

- **Super Smash Bros. Ultimate**: Features one of the largest game soundtracks ever assembled, with hundreds of music tracks that can be assigned to individual stages. Sakurai personally supervised volume balancing across different sound effect types.
- **Kirby series**: Each game features memorable, mood-appropriate music that shifts with gameplay contexts (peaceful overworld vs. intense boss battles).
- **Sound Effect Design**: In Smash Bros., the crispness and timing of hit sounds work in concert with hitstop to create satisfying impact feedback.

### Review Checkpoints

- Does the audio design match the mood and pacing of the gameplay?
- Are sound effects distinctive, satisfying, and informative?
- Does the music tempo match the game's pace at each moment?
- Is the audio mix balanced (no element drowns out others)?
- Would the game be significantly worse with the sound muted? (If not, audio is under-leveraged.)

## 9. Visual Feedback and Polish

### Principle

Every frame of animation and every visual effect should serve a purpose. Techniques like additive blending, center-of-gravity animation, and careful visual effects design make games feel alive and responsive.

### Explanation

Sakurai pays extraordinary attention to animation polish. Key techniques include:

- **Center of Gravity Shifting**: Altering the center of gravity of animated objects makes them appear more multidimensional, prominent, and impactful. For example, animating cannonballs with a six-frame wobble as they travel instead of pure horizontal movement.
- **Additive Blending**: A rendering technique that helps visual effects line up stylistically with backgrounds. However, additive blending alone is insufficient; proper contrast and style considerations are also necessary.
- **Exaggerated Animation**: Contorting characters beyond realistic limits (like bending Kazuya's spine backward) to make animations feel more dynamic and impactful.

### Application Examples

- **Super Smash Bros.**: Visual effects for attacks use additive blending and are visible even in slow motion, with each frame carefully designed.
- **Smash Bros. UI**: Icon sizes are deliberately varied based on importance, creating visual hierarchy.
- **General Philosophy**: Making every last frame of every animation stand out, contributing to the feeling that the game is alive.

### Review Checkpoints

- Do visual effects communicate gameplay information clearly?
- Are animations exaggerated appropriately to enhance game feel?
- Is there visual hierarchy (important elements are more prominent)?
- Do effects maintain readability at game speed?
- Does the visual polish enhance the player's sense of feedback and responsiveness?

## 10. UI Clarity and Communication

### Principle

User interfaces should be understandable without words whenever possible. Visual hierarchy, clear toggle states, and minimal complexity are essential for effective UI.

> "Whenever possible, it's best to make your menus understandable even without the use of words."

### Explanation

Sakurai stresses that UI design must serve the player's ability to navigate and understand the game. Key principles include: varying icon sizes based on importance, ensuring toggle states (on/off) are perfectly clear (preferring checkboxes over toggles), keeping menus simple to avoid confusion, and using font and color choices deliberately to enhance perception. He has cited Persona 5 as his favorite example of stylish game UI design.

### Application Examples

- **Super Smash Bros. Ultimate**: Character select screen communicates vast amounts of information through visual hierarchy. Damage percentages are instantly readable during chaotic four-player battles.
- **Kid Icarus: Uprising**: Weapon stats are presented visually with clear comparisons, allowing players to make quick decisions.
- **General Principle**: Simply adjusting fonts and colors of a standard settings menu can make a significant impact on how the game is perceived overall.

### Review Checkpoints

- Can the UI be understood without reading text?
- Are important elements visually larger or more prominent than secondary ones?
- Are toggle states (on/off, selected/unselected) immediately clear?
- Is the menu structure simple enough to avoid confusion?
- Does the UI communicate essential information during active gameplay without breaking flow?

## 11. The Concept is Sacred

### Principle

A game's concept is its core direction and most important asset. If development strays from the concept due to unexpected challenges, the project will lose focus. Do not change the concept just because reality diverges from expectations.

> "A game's concept is one of its most important assets. The concept is the core that directs a game's appeal. If you change it just because things diverged from your expectations, the project will lose its way."

### Explanation

Before development begins, the concept defines what the game is about, what experience it delivers, and why it is compelling. Sakurai can envision a game down to the smallest detail before starting work. When difficulties arise during development, the temptation is to change the concept to match what is easier to build. Sakurai warns against this, arguing that maintaining the concept through adversity is what produces coherent, focused games.

### Application Examples

- **Kirby's Dream Land**: The concept was "a game that anyone could enjoy." This guided every design decision from control scheme to difficulty.
- **Super Smash Bros.**: The concept of a crossover fighting game accessible to non-fighting-game players guided every decision, from simplified inputs to the party-game format.
- **Super Smash Bros. Ultimate**: The "Everyone Is Here" concept meant including every single character, despite the enormous development burden.

### Review Checkpoints

- Is the game's core concept clearly defined and documented?
- Do all design decisions trace back to and support the core concept?
- Has the concept been preserved through development challenges, or has it drifted?
- Can the concept be communicated in one or two sentences?
- Would a player, after five minutes, understand what the game's concept is?

## 12. Resource Allocation and Efficiency

### Principle

Identify where effort produces the greatest impact and allocate resources there. Not all features deserve equal investment. Cut or minimize work on low-impact areas and concentrate on what makes the most difference to the player experience.

> "Identify where the effect is high and where it is low. Allocate resources to the most effective areas."

### Explanation

Game development has finite resources (time, budget, personnel). Sakurai emphasizes a disciplined approach to prioritization: determine which features and polish areas will have the greatest impact on the player's experience, and invest heavily there. Areas with diminishing returns should receive less attention. This is not about cutting corners but about strategic allocation in service of the best possible final product.

### Application Examples

- **Super Smash Bros. Ultimate**: Despite including 80+ characters and 100+ stages, Sakurai's team focused polish on the areas most visible and impactful to players (character animations, hit feedback, stage interactions).
- **General Development**: Sakurai discusses how producers can streamline the development pipeline and lead projects to successful launches by finding solutions from multiple perspectives.

### Review Checkpoints

- Are development resources focused on high-impact areas?
- Are there features receiving disproportionate effort relative to their impact?
- Has the team identified which elements players will notice most?
- Are low-impact features simplified or cut rather than consuming equal resources?
- Is there a clear priority ranking for features and polish?

## 13. Team Communication and Shared Vision

### Principle

Game development requires effective information sharing across the entire team. Every member should understand the project's direction, and communication channels should minimize information loss.

> "Without separating by designers or programmers, bringing as many staff members as possible in and sharing the same information among us. By doing that, we can limit the risk of information getting lost on the way."

### Explanation

Sakurai advocates for inclusive communication where all team members, regardless of role, share the same information pool. The general image of the project should be visible from the very first business proposal through to the final product, so that the entire team works under a unified vision. Daily or weekly reports create a continuous stream of information. When team members operate in information silos, misalignment compounds over time, producing a game that feels inconsistent.

### Application Examples

- **General Practice**: Sakurai shares design documents (with Nintendo's permission) and provides templates for presentations and game proposals.
- **Development Process**: He emphasizes clearly defining the direction for the whole team and then moving forward under a unified vision.

### Review Checkpoints

- Does every team member understand the project's core concept and direction?
- Are information-sharing mechanisms (meetings, reports, documents) in place?
- Is communication cross-disciplinary (not siloed by role)?
- Are design documents accessible to the entire team?
- Is the team's understanding of the game's vision consistent?

## Consolidated Design Review Checklist

A quick-reference checklist for reviewing game designs using Sakurai's principles.

### Core Game Feel

- [ ] Risk-and-reward loop is present and clear
- [ ] Tension builds and releases in satisfying cycles
- [ ] Core fun is apparent within the first three minutes
- [ ] Game is accessible to beginners while offering depth for experts
- [ ] Controls are simple to learn, with complexity revealed gradually
- [ ] Player perspective drives all design decisions

### Balance and Identity

- [ ] Game elements (characters, weapons, etc.) feel distinct and unique
- [ ] Strengths are preserved; weaknesses are used to offset, not flattened
- [ ] Players can choose options that match their playstyle

### Feedback and Polish

- [ ] Impactful actions have hitstop/freeze and appropriate visual-audio feedback
- [ ] Audio design is distinctive, satisfying, and matches pacing
- [ ] Visual effects communicate gameplay information clearly
- [ ] UI is understandable without text where possible
- [ ] Animations feel alive with weight and exaggeration

### Design Integrity

- [ ] Core concept is clearly defined and consistently followed
- [ ] No major design drift from the original concept
- [ ] Sound design elevates the experience (not just decorative)

### Production

- [ ] Resources are allocated to highest-impact areas
- [ ] Team shares a unified vision and information flows freely
- [ ] Low-impact features are deprioritized, not over-invested
