# Literary and Media Influences on Hidetaka Miyazaki's Game Design

A reference documenting the specific works, genres, and creators that shaped Miyazaki's design philosophy, with analysis of how each influence manifests in his games. Sourced from interviews (2009-2024), the Game Informer 2015 profile, the Rolling Stone/Glixel 2016 bookshelf tour, and press features.

---

## 1. The Childhood Reading Experience (Origin of Fragmentary Design)

### The Foundation

Miyazaki grew up in a low-income household in a small town in Japan, where his parents could not afford books or manga. He frequented his local library, borrowing whatever he could find -- including English-language fantasy and texts with kanji far beyond his reading level. He could only comprehend roughly half of what he read.

> "When I was young, I used to enjoy reading books that were too hard for me, where I could only read maybe half the kanji, and using my imagination to fill in the gaps. I wanted to see if I could bring that kind of experience to a video game."
> -- PlayStation Blog, 2018

> "Growing up, as a kid, I loved to read. I liked to read books that were above my range. I always tried to aim higher and read difficult books."

This act of imagination -- filling gaps in incomplete information -- became the single most important principle in Miyazaki's design philosophy. Every aspect of his games, from fragmentary narrative to obscured lore to hidden paths, traces back to this childhood experience.

### Design Principle Derived

**Incomplete information engages the imagination more deeply than complete information.** When the reader/player must fill in gaps, they become a co-creator of the experience. The resulting personal interpretation is more memorable and meaningful than anything the author could have explicitly provided.

### Manifestation in Games

| Experience | Game Design Equivalent |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Reading half-understood English text | Item descriptions that hint but never fully explain |
| Imagining illustrations for text-only pages | Environmental storytelling where architecture implies narrative |
| Piecing together partial kanji meanings | Lore fragments distributed across hundreds of items |
| Re-reading passages for new understanding | New Game+ revealing previously missed connections |
| The joy of suddenly comprehending a passage | "Aha moments" when lore pieces click together |

## 2. Fighting Fantasy Gamebooks (Structural Blueprint)

### Overview

Fighting Fantasy is a series of single-player gamebooks created by Steve Jackson and Ian Livingstone, first published by Puffin Books in 1982. The first title was *The Warlock of Firetop Mountain*. Around 15 million copies were sold between 1982 and 1995.

Each book is divided into approximately 400 numbered sections. The reader starts at section 1 and makes choices that lead to non-sequential sections. Characters are created by rolling dice for three statistics: Skill, Stamina, and Luck. Combat uses a dice-plus-Skill comparison system, with Stamina serving as hit points.

### Miyazaki's Specific Connection

> "The roots of my fantasy ideas are in Sorcery, by Ian Livingstone and Steve Jackson, the adventure game books."
> -- GameSpot, 2015

> "There was a board game called Sorcery which is one of my favorites and I would often revisit the game. It definitely stands out in my mind as a game that impacted me."
> -- IGN, 2018

> "You might say that trying to capture the excitement of those old tabletop games and game books was one facet of making Elden Ring."
> -- PC Gamer, 2024

Miyazaki was restricted from playing video games until university. Gamebooks and tabletop RPGs were his primary gaming experiences throughout childhood and adolescence. At the 2018 Golden Joystick Awards, his Lifetime Achievement Award was presented by Steve Jackson and Ian Livingstone themselves.

His bookshelf (documented in the Rolling Stone/Glixel 2016 tour) prominently displays the Sorcery! series alongside the companion guides *Titan* (the world guide) and *Out of the Pit* (the bestiary).

### Steve Jackson's Sorcery! Series (Primary Influence)

The Sorcery! series (1983-1985) is a four-part gamebook saga set within the Fighting Fantasy universe:

1. *The Shamutanti Hills*
2. *Khare -- Cityport of Traps*
3. *The Seven Serpents*
4. *The Crown of Kings*

Key features that map directly to Souls design:

| Sorcery! Feature | Souls Design Equivalent |
|-----------------|----------------------|
| **48 memorizable three-letter spell codes** (ZAP, HOT, etc.) | Arcane game knowledge shared within community; hidden mechanics |
| **Spells cost Stamina** (which doubles as hit points) | Resource management where offensive and defensive resources overlap |
| **Cross-book continuity** (same character, carried items/abilities) | New Game+; cross-game lore connections; carried knowledge |
| **Hidden clues in early books aid later situations** | Early-game items/lore that only make sense in late-game context |
| **Multiple paths, many leading to death** | Branching level design with dead ends and hidden optimal routes |

### Structural Parallels: Gamebook Genre to Souls Design

| Gamebook Element | Souls Design Equivalent |
|-----------------|----------------------|
| **Numbered paragraph exploration** | Room-based level design evolving into interconnected worlds |
| **Gaps between paragraphs** (reader imagines the transition) | Fragmentary narrative requiring player interpretation |
| **Item descriptions** (brief flavor text for found objects) | Item descriptions as primary lore delivery system |
| **"You are dead. Start over."** (frequent death, restart) | Death-retry loop with souls/runes recovery |
| **Lone adventurer in hostile territory** | Solitary protagonist in a hostile world |
| **Hidden optimal paths** (discovered through re-reads) | Secret areas, illusory walls, hidden questlines |
| **Limited choices creating meaningful decisions** | Constrained but weighty player decisions |
| **Dungeon-crawling, trap-avoiding structure** | Legacy dungeon design (Sen's Fortress, Stormveil Castle) |
| **Inventory affecting available choices** | Key items unlocking new paths and interactions |
| **The "try again" culture** | Death as a learning mechanism, not a failure state |

### Key Fighting Fantasy Titles

| Title | Year | Relevance to Souls Design |
|-------|------|--------------------------|
| *The Warlock of Firetop Mountain* | 1982 | The template: dungeon crawl, lone hero, hidden treasure |
| *The Citadel of Chaos* | 1983 | Assault on a wizard's fortress; magic system |
| *City of Thieves* | 1983 | Urban adventure in dangerous Port Blacksand; NPC interactions |
| *Deathtrap Dungeon* | 1984 | Lethal competition dungeon; trap design as gameplay |
| *House of Hell* | 1984 | Horror-themed; atmosphere and dread as primary experience |
| *Creature of Havoc* | 1986 | Experimental narrative where the reader starts as a confused monster |
| *Sorcery! series* | 1983-85 | Miyazaki's cited primary influence; spell system, cross-book continuity |

### RuneQuest (Cited as Key Influence on Elden Ring)

> Miyazaki specifically cited RuneQuest as "a key influence" on Elden Ring.
> -- Chaosium blog

RuneQuest (first published 1978 by Chaosium) is a tabletop RPG set in the mythological world of Glorantha. Key features that map to Miyazaki's design:

- **Skill-based progression** (not class-based): Characters improve by using skills, not by accumulating generic experience points. This mirrors the Souls approach where player skill matters more than character level.
- **Deadly combat**: RuneQuest combat is lethal and tactical, with hit locations, armor by body part, and the real possibility of character death in any encounter.
- **Rich mythology**: Glorantha's lore is delivered through fragmented, culturally biased in-world texts rather than omniscient exposition.
- **Cults and factions**: Characters join religious cults that shape their abilities and worldview, paralleling the covenant systems in Dark Souls.

### Design Principles Derived from Tabletop

| Tabletop Principle | Souls Design Manifestation |
|-------------------|---------------------------|
| **Character builds as identity** | Diverse build paths (strength, dexterity, magic, faith) |
| **Dice-roll unpredictability** | Enemy behavior variation; critical hits; status effects |
| **Collaborative imagination** | Community lore interpretation; player messages |
| **Game Master as world curator** | Miyazaki as director who designs the world, not the player's path |
| **Session-based play** | Bonfire-to-bonfire progression segments |

### Miyazaki's Unfulfilled Desire

Miyazaki has expressed interest in creating tabletop RPGs and card games himself, but was "specifically ordered by our previous president to not work on this until I have retired."

## 4. George R.R. Martin (Narrative Peer)

### A Song of Ice and Fire: Narrative Parallels

| Martin's Technique | Miyazaki's Design Equivalent |
|-------------------|----------------------------|
| **Multiple unreliable perspectives** | Lore told through biased item descriptions and NPC dialogue |
| **Characters die without warning** | No plot armor; NPCs can die permanently through player action or inaction |
| **Moral ambiguity** | No clear heroes or villains; bosses are tragic as often as threatening |
| **History-based worldbuilding** | Lore delivered through accumulated fragments of the world's past |
| **Harsh, unforgiving world** | The core design principle: the world is harsh, but persistence has meaning |
| **Subverted expectations** | Hidden truths that recontextualize the player's understanding |

### Fevre Dream (Miyazaki's Favorite Martin Novel)

*Fevre Dream* (1982) is a vampire novel set on the antebellum Mississippi River. A steamboat captain partners with a mysterious figure who turns out to be a vampire seeking to free his kind from their bloodlust through an alchemical potion. The ancient villain represents corruption and stagnation opposing redemption.

> "I personally see Fevre Dream as a masterpiece among vampire fantasy."
> -- Miyazaki (E3 2019 era)

Miyazaki reportedly recommended this novel to all new FromSoftware employees. His admiration for *Fevre Dream* -- not the more famous *A Song of Ice and Fire* -- was a primary motivator for wanting to collaborate with Martin. Thematic parallels have been drawn between the novel and Bloodborne's Gothic aesthetic, as well as between the character Joshua York and Elden Ring's Miquella.

### The Elden Ring Collaboration

The collaboration was structured to preserve both creators' strengths:

1. **Miyazaki gave Martin "very vague and broad themes and ideas for the mythos"** -- abstract creative direction, consistent with how he directs his own team.
2. **Martin wrote the historical foundation** -- a textual world-building document with no visuals, detailing prehistory, the flow of time, and key figures.
3. **Martin was NOT asked to write in-game text or the game's story** -- "because we felt that would actually limit his creative output."
4. **FromSoftware then fragmented Martin's work** -- Characters who were "much closer to human form" in Martin's writing became corrupted demigods. The mythology was distributed across item descriptions, environmental details, and NPC dialogue.
5. **Martin attributed "almost all the credit" for Elden Ring's success to Miyazaki and his team.**

This process exemplifies Miyazaki's abstract creative direction principle applied to an external collaborator: provide emotional/thematic seed material, then trust the creative partner to produce something richer than specific instructions would yield.

## 5. Berserk by Kentaro Miura (Aesthetic Foundation)

### Overview

Berserk is a dark fantasy manga by Kentaro Miura, serialized from 1989. It follows Guts, a lone mercenary wielding an enormous sword, who struggles against demonic forces in a medieval world of extreme violence, tragedy, and moments of profound beauty. When Berserk began serialization, Miyazaki was 15 years old.

Miyazaki has acknowledged the influence in the Dark Souls Design Works interview. His personal shelf prominently features Berserk alongside Devilman.

### Thematic Parallels

| Berserk Theme | Souls Design Manifestation |
|--------------|---------------------------|
| **Lone warrior against overwhelming odds** | The solitary player character in a hostile world |
| **Beauty within darkness** | The "dignified grotesque" aesthetic principle |
| **Persistence despite suffering** | Death-retry loop as expression of the will to continue |
| **Cursed mark binding the protagonist** | Darksign (Dark Souls), Hunter's Mark (Bloodborne) |
| **Bonds formed in adversity** | Summoning phantoms; NPC companions |
| **The monstrous as tragic** | Bosses who embody contradiction (fearsome yet pitiable) |

### Visual References by Game

| Game | Reference | Berserk Source |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| Demon's Souls | Dragon Bone Smasher | Dragon Slayer sword |
| Dark Souls | Artorias of the Abyss (DLC cover) | Berserk Volume 28 cover; Berserker Armor |
| Dark Souls | The Greatsword (weapon) | Guts' Dragon Slayer |
| Dark Souls | Wheel Skeletons | Medieval breaking wheels in Berserk |
| Dark Souls | Darksign | Brand of Sacrifice |
| Dark Souls III | Sulyvahn Beast | Beast of Darkness |
| Dark Souls III | Slave Knight Gael (pose) | Guts' post-battle stance |
| Bloodborne | Hunter's Mark | Brand of Sacrifice |
| Sekiro | Owl/Wolf relationship | Gambino/Guts mentorship |

### What Berserk Provided

Berserk gave Miyazaki the visual and emotional vocabulary for his dark fantasy worlds: the enormous swords, the cursed protagonists, the demonic transformations, and above all, the principle that darkness must contain dignity and sorrow rather than mere shock value. Miura's ability to depict horror while maintaining empathy for his characters directly informs Miyazaki's "dignified grotesque" aesthetic.

## 6. Lovecraft, Stoker, and Gothic Horror (Bloodborne's DNA)

### H.P. Lovecraft

Lovecraft's influence is most visible in Bloodborne, where the game begins as Gothic horror (vampires, werewolves, Victorian architecture) and gradually reveals cosmic horror (eldritch beings, madness, insignificance of humanity). Key Lovecraftian elements:

- **Cosmic indifference**: The Great Ones in Bloodborne are not evil -- they are simply beyond human comprehension
- **Forbidden knowledge**: The Insight mechanic literalizes Lovecraft's theme that knowing too much about reality drives you mad
- **Transformation through understanding**: As the player gains Insight, the world literally changes -- previously invisible cosmic entities become visible
- **The insignificance of humanity**: The entire Yharnam crisis is a side effect of beings who barely notice human existence

### Bram Stoker

Stoker's *Dracula* and the broader Victorian Gothic tradition inform Bloodborne's aesthetic: the gaslit streets, the plague-ravaged populace, the aristocratic monsters, and the thin veneer of civilization over primal horror. Miyazaki has cited both Lovecraft and Stoker as influences on Bloodborne.

## 7. Western Fantasy Literature (Epic Scaffolding)

### J.R.R. Tolkien

The Tolkien connection operates on two levels:

1. **Childhood experience**: Miyazaki read Tolkien as a child, struggling with the English text. The experience of partially comprehending a vast fantasy world -- understanding enough to sense its depth but not enough to grasp every detail -- directly inspired his fragmentary narrative approach.

2. **World-building philosophy**: Both Tolkien and Miyazaki build worlds through accumulated history, language, and cultural artifact rather than direct exposition. Neither creator moralizes overtly; the world simply exists, and the audience infers meaning.

### Michael Moorcock's *The Eternal Champion*

Specifically cited by Miyazaki as an influence on Elden Ring's creative process, alongside Tolkien and RuneQuest. Moorcock's multiverse concept -- where the same eternal hero appears across different worlds and times -- resonates with the cyclical nature of the Souls universe (the linking of the fire, the cycle of Ages).

## 9. Convergence: How All Influences Create the "Miyazaki Aesthetic"

The through-line connecting all influences is **imagination filling gaps in incomplete information**:

| Influence | What It Provided |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Childhood reading** | The philosophy: incomplete comprehension engages imagination more deeply |
| **Fighting Fantasy** | The structure: paragraph-based exploration, death-retry loops, hidden paths |
| **Tabletop RPGs** | The systems: character builds, stat management, collaborative imagination |
| **Berserk** | The aesthetic: beauty within darkness, dignity within grotesquerie |
| **George R.R. Martin** | The narrative peer: history-based worldbuilding, moral ambiguity, harsh worlds |
| **Lovecraft / Stoker** | The genre vocabulary: cosmic horror, Gothic atmosphere, forbidden knowledge |
| **Tolkien / Moorcock** | The epic scaffolding: deep history, cyclical narratives, world as artifact |
| **Manga** | The visual reference: equipment design, character naming, Japanese dark fantasy |

Each influence contributes a distinct layer, but they all serve the same core principle: **the player's imagination is the most powerful tool in the designer's arsenal.** By providing fragments rather than completeness, Miyazaki transforms the player from a passive consumer into an active co-creator of the experience.
