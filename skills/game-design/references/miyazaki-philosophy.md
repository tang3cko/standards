# Hidetaka Miyazaki's Game Design Philosophy

A comprehensive reference of design principles from Hidetaka Miyazaki (president of FromSoftware, director of Demon's Souls, Dark Souls, Bloodborne, Sekiro, and Elden Ring), sourced from interviews, the Dark Souls Design Works book, GDC-adjacent coverage, and press features (2009-2024).

---

## 1. Accomplishment Through Adversity (Core Design Goal)

### Principle

The fundamental goal of game design is to provide a profound sense of accomplishment. Difficulty is not the objective itself but a means to deliver that feeling. Players must overcome genuine obstacles to experience genuine triumph.

> "Having the game be 'difficult' was never the goal. What we set out to do was strictly to provide a sense of accomplishment. We understood that 'difficulty' is just one way to offer an intense sense of accomplishment through forming strategies, overcoming obstacles, and discovering new things."
> -- Game Informer, 2009 (Demon's Souls)

### Explanation

The Japanese concept of 達成感 (tatsukan -- sense of achievement) is the central pillar of Miyazaki's design. Every system, every enemy placement, every level layout exists to serve this goal. The difficulty is calibrated not to punish but to make the eventual success feel earned. Miyazaki draws a precise line between "difficult" and "unreasonable": if the player dies and can understand why, that is fair design; if the death feels arbitrary or unjustified, it is unreasonable and must be corrected.

> "It's not a matter of simply cranking up the difficulty. It's doing so fairly. When players are killed, and they can understand why they were killed in an instance, and it feels justified -- 'Okay, yeah, that makes sense' -- that's the game design we're trying to achieve."
> -- Game Informer, 2024

> "The term I usually use is 'unreasonable.'"
> -- Game Informer, 2024

### Application Examples

- **Demon's Souls**: The entire game was built around the sense of accomplishment from the earliest stages of development, with difficulty as one tool among several to achieve it.
- **Dark Souls - Ornstein and Smough**: A two-boss encounter that tests everything the player has learned about spacing, patience, and stamina management. Victory produces intense catharsis precisely because the challenge is steep but fair.
- **Elden Ring**: Rather than lowering difficulty, accessibility is provided through open-world freedom. Players can leave a challenge, explore elsewhere, level up, and return. The accomplishment is preserved; the path to it is flexible.
- **Sekiro - Genichiro Ashina**: A skill gate that forces players to master the deflection system. The encounter teaches through repeated failure, and the eventual victory marks a turning point in the player's understanding.

### Review Checkpoints

- Does overcoming challenges produce a genuine sense of accomplishment?
- When the player fails, can they understand why and what to do differently?
- Is difficulty serving the experience, or is it difficulty for its own sake?
- Are there moments where the player can feel "I did it!" with real emotional weight?
- Does the game avoid "unreasonable" deaths (arbitrary, unjustified, unlearnable)?

## 2. Meaningful Death (Death as Design Tool)

### Principle

Death in games should not be merely a failure state. It should carry meaning, teach the player, and become an integral part of the gameplay loop. The question is not how to avoid player death but how to make death itself a valuable experience.

> "I've never been a very skilled player. I die a lot. So, in my work, I want to answer the question: If death is to be more than a mark of failure, how do I give it meaning? How do I make death enjoyable?"
> -- The New Yorker, 2022

### Explanation

Miyazaki's personal relationship with difficulty -- he openly admits to being bad at games -- informs this principle. Every death should provide a learning opportunity: pattern recognition, spatial awareness, mechanical understanding. The souls/runes recovery mechanic transforms death from a binary fail state into a risk-reward loop where the player's corpse becomes a goal to reach. Death also serves a narrative function, reinforcing the harsh world and the player's perseverance within it.

### Application Examples

- **Dark Souls - Souls Recovery**: Dying drops the player's souls at their death location. They get one chance to recover them. This transforms death into a tense sub-objective and adds stakes without being permanently punishing.
- **Bloodborne - Insight System**: Death and discovery feed into the Insight mechanic, which literally changes what the player can perceive in the world. Death becomes a form of progression.
- **Sekiro - Dragonrot**: Repeated deaths have consequences for NPCs, creating narrative weight to failure while still allowing the player to continue.
- **Elden Ring - Stakes of Maricka**: Checkpoints placed near difficult encounters reduce the friction of repeated death while maintaining the loop of attempt, learn, and overcome.

### Review Checkpoints

- Does death teach the player something actionable?
- Is there a meaningful consequence to death beyond restarting?
- Does the death-retry loop maintain engagement rather than producing frustration?
- Can the player feel progress even through repeated failures?
- Does the death mechanic serve the game's themes and world?

## 3. Fragmentary Narrative (Storytelling Through Gaps)

### Principle

Stories are most powerful when they are incomplete. Deliberate gaps in narrative invite player imagination, creating a more personal and memorable experience than explicit exposition. The world tells its story through fragments -- item descriptions, architecture, environmental details, and NPC behavior -- never through direct delivery.

> "The truth is, I'm just not good at implementing direct narrative in my games."
> -- WIRED, 2016

> "There are a huge number of things that while present in the game, we make no attempt to explain to the player, and many more that they simply have no way of finding out."
> -- Dark Souls Design Works Interview

### Explanation

This philosophy originates from Miyazaki's childhood experience of reading books beyond his comprehension level -- English texts and kanji-heavy literature where he could only understand roughly half the content. He filled in the gaps with his imagination, and this act of imaginative participation became more engaging than complete understanding would have been. He deliberately replicates this dynamic in his games: narrative is distributed across item descriptions, architectural details, enemy placements, and environmental storytelling, leaving players to piece together meaning.

The approach follows the principle of "story for the game" rather than "game for the story." Narrative serves the gameplay experience; it should never constrain it.

> "Dark Souls is in some ways an incomplete game, and I like to think that it has been completed by players, by their discoveries, as they moved along."
> -- GamesRadar

### Application Examples

- **Dark Souls - Item Descriptions**: Every weapon, ring, and armor piece carries a fragment of lore. The Sunlight Straight Sword's description tells the story of Solaire without a single cutscene.
- **Bloodborne - Architecture**: The transition from Victorian streets to eldritch nightmare is told through the changing architecture, not through exposition.
- **Elden Ring - George R. R. Martin Collaboration**: Martin wrote the overarching mythology (set long before the game's events), which Miyazaki then fragmented and distributed throughout the world. The collaboration preserved both Martin's creative output and Miyazaki's fragmentary method.
- **Dark Souls - Undead Asylum**: The opening area teaches the player about the world's hopelessness and the cycle of undeath through environmental design alone.

### Review Checkpoints

- Is narrative delivered through discovery rather than exposition?
- Do item descriptions, environments, and NPC behavior carry lore?
- Are there intentional gaps that invite player interpretation?
- Does the player feel rewarded for paying attention to details?
- Can the game's story be enjoyed at multiple depths (surface-level and deep investigation)?

## 4. Interconnected World Design (Spatial Narrative)

### Principle

The topology of the game world -- how spaces connect, where shortcuts emerge, how verticality creates surprise -- is itself a primary narrative and emotional tool. Spaces should loop back on themselves, creating moments of revelation when the player discovers unexpected connections.

> "I have always taken a very heavy involvement in stage design and level design because I want to create those exact experiences... the map and level design are supposed to serve as this guide to help players pick up more information and piece together what they think that world is."
> -- Game Informer, 2024

### Explanation

Miyazaki's world design follows the principle: "If we can connect it, we will." Rather than treating levels as isolated missions, the world is conceived as one interconnected entity. A path that could be reached in a straight line is deliberately made into a circle, so that looping back produces a moment of surprise and satisfaction. Vertical structure creates a sense of freedom within limited space, and shortcuts serve as emotional rewards -- the relief of opening a shortcut is proportional to the difficulty of the path that led to it.

> "After ringing the bells and overcoming the traps of Sen's Fortress I really wanted the player to feel 'Yes! I've made it!'"
> -- Design Works Interview

### Application Examples

- **Dark Souls 1 - Lordran**: The gold standard of interconnected world design. The elevator from Undead Parish to Firelink Shrine is one of gaming's most celebrated spatial revelations.
- **Dark Souls 1 - Vertical Structure**: Firelink Shrine sits at the center; paths lead up to Undead Burg, down to New Londo Ruins, and across to various areas, all physically connected in three-dimensional space.
- **Elden Ring - Open World Evolution**: The interconnected philosophy was adapted for open-world design. The Guidance of Grace system prevents decision paralysis while preserving exploration freedom. Legacy Dungeons within Elden Ring retain the classic interconnected design.
- **Elden Ring - Shadow of the Erdtree**: Miyazaki blended open-field and legacy-dungeon approaches, creating "a better sense of verticality" in the DLC.

### Review Checkpoints

- Do areas connect in ways that create moments of spatial revelation?
- Are shortcuts designed as emotional rewards for exploration?
- Does the world feel like a cohesive whole rather than disconnected levels?
- Is vertical space used to create a sense of scale and discovery?
- Does exploring the world itself tell a story about the setting?

## 5. Discovery and the Unknown (Player Agency)

### Principle

For something to be a discovery, it must first be unknown. Games should contain genuine mysteries, hidden paths, and obscured information that reward curiosity and investigation. The designer should create spaces for players to enjoy the game in ways that were not explicitly authored.

> "You need to have some unknowns. And for it to be a discovery, it needs to feel like it's an unknown, feel like it's there to be discovered."

> "I try to make a game that has beautiful open spaces, gaps, room for players to enjoy it in ways that were not authored. I never want it to be where you have to follow the rules completely, where you have to do things exactly as the designers intended."

### Explanation

Miyazaki designs worlds where NPCs are inhabitants with their own agendas, not quest-givers serving the player. Their stories happen whether the player witnesses them or not. This makes the world feel lived-in rather than staged. Information is intentionally obscured so that uncovering it becomes an achievement. The multiplayer system extends this philosophy: player-left messages and bloodstains create a web of indirect, community-driven discovery.

### Application Examples

- **Dark Souls - Solaire's Quest**: It is impossible to complete Solaire's story without external hints. His survival is effectively a secret for dedicated players, and his default fate (being consumed by the Sunlight Maggot) is the expected outcome.
- **Dark Souls - Illusory Walls**: Hidden passages that reward players who experiment with hitting walls, creating a culture of exploration and community knowledge-sharing.
- **Bloodborne - Upper Cathedral Ward**: An entire area that many players never find, containing critical lore about the game's deepest mysteries.
- **Elden Ring - Hidden Areas**: Numerous areas accessible only through obscure means (invisible bridges, hidden teleporters, quest-locked passages).

### Review Checkpoints

- Are there genuine secrets and hidden content that reward curiosity?
- Do NPCs behave as world inhabitants rather than player-serving quest dispensers?
- Is there room for players to discover things in unintended ways?
- Does the community naturally form around sharing discoveries?
- Is obscurity used purposefully (to reward investigation) rather than arbitrarily?

## 6. Indirect Multiplayer (Silent Cooperation)

### Principle

Multiplayer interaction can be profound without direct communication. Asynchronous, non-verbal systems create connection between players while preserving the solitary atmosphere of the world.

### Explanation

The origin of this philosophy is a personal experience. Miyazaki's car became stuck on an icy hill, and he watched as a line of drivers silently pushed one another's cars out of the snow before departing without exchanging a word. He wondered whether he would have been friends with these people if they had met face-to-face, and realized a game could replicate this form of connection. He calls this concept "mutual assistance between transient people."

The bonfire system embodies this: it shows the vague shadows of other online players resting at the same spot, providing "emotional relief" and the sense that "they're not alone in this endeavor."

### Application Examples

- **Dark Souls - Messages**: Players leave hints for others using a limited vocabulary system. These messages can be rated, creating a feedback loop of anonymous help.
- **Dark Souls - Bloodstains**: Touching a bloodstain replays the final moments of another player's death, serving as a warning and a source of dark humor.
- **Dark Souls - Phantoms**: Ghostly silhouettes of other players appear briefly, showing what they are doing in the same area. No direct interaction occurs, but the presence is felt.
- **Dark Souls - Summoning**: Cooperative play is framed as summoning a phantom from another world -- temporary, wordless assistance that preserves the game's lonely atmosphere.

### Review Checkpoints

- Can players feel the presence of others without direct communication?
- Do asynchronous systems create meaningful connections between players?
- Does multiplayer interaction preserve the game's intended atmosphere?
- Is indirect cooperation rewarding for both the helper and the helped?
- Does the multiplayer system generate community culture naturally?

## 7. Dignified Grotesque (Aesthetic Philosophy)

### Principle

Dark and horrifying imagery should convey emotional depth -- sorrow, dignity, tragedy -- rather than surface-level shock. Beauty requires something ugly, depraved, or tragic to heighten and embolden it. Refinement, elegance, and dignity must be maintained even in the most grotesque designs.

> "This isn't dignified. Don't rely on the gross factor to portray an undead dragon. Can't you instead try to convey the deep sorrow of a magnificent beast doomed to a slow and possibly endless descent into ruin?"
> -- Dark Souls Design Works Interview

> "Something beautiful needs something ugly -- something that's depraved or tragic to heighten and embolden that beauty."
> -- PC Gamer, 2024

> "I always tried to maintain a certain level of refinement and elegance in all designs. Muddy or messy is definitely not good."
> -- Design Works Interview

### Explanation

Miyazaki's aesthetic philosophy rejects pure horror or shock value. Every creature, environment, and character should evoke empathy on a deeper level. The undead dragon should make you feel the sorrow of a once-magnificent beast, not just revulsion. This principle extends to all visual design: "Flat-out grotesque or splatter type designs will not get past me." The result is a distinctive visual identity where beauty and horror coexist, each amplifying the other.

His influences -- Berserk (Kentaro Miura), European cathedrals, Western dark fantasy literature -- all share this quality of finding beauty within darkness.

### Application Examples

- **Dark Souls - Sif, the Great Grey Wolf**: A boss fight that becomes tragic when the player discovers Sif's backstory. The wolf limps when near death, creating an emotional gut-punch through animation alone.
- **Bloodborne - Vicar Amelia**: A beast that prays even in its monstrous form, conveying the tragedy of a devout servant transformed beyond recognition.
- **Dark Souls - Anor Londo**: A city of divine beauty that conceals a deep illusion, where the grandeur itself becomes a source of sorrow once the truth is revealed.
- **Elden Ring - Malenia**: A warrior whose power literally decomposes her body. Strength and decay are inseparable, embodying the "dignified grotesque" principle.

### Review Checkpoints

- Do dark or horrifying elements convey emotional depth beyond shock value?
- Is there dignity and sorrow in the game's antagonists and monsters?
- Does the visual design maintain refinement even in grotesque subjects?
- Do beauty and horror coexist and amplify each other?
- Can the player empathize with or feel something deeper about the enemies they face?

## 8. Contradiction in Boss Design

### Principle

The most memorable bosses embody contradiction -- they are fearsome yet sorrowful, powerful yet pitiable, monstrous yet dignified. Boss design should communicate lore and emotion, not just serve as combat challenges.

> "One of the elements I want to bring to boss character design is contradiction... not just fearful enemies but something more that can be sensed from each boss character."
> -- Gamedeveloper.com

### Explanation

Miyazaki designs bosses around a "heroic concept" -- characters who were once noble or heroic but have been warped by circumstance, trauma, or the inability to handle power. The emotional arc of a boss fight should include not just the thrill of combat but a deeper response: sadness, respect, or understanding. Boss design begins early in area development, with the boss's gameplay forming a premise for the surrounding level design.

His greatest regret, the Bed of Chaos, represents a failure of this philosophy: a boss that felt neither fair nor emotionally resonant, exposing flaws in the production process.

### Application Examples

- **Dark Souls - Dancer of the Frigid Valley**: "She is definitely a formidable enemy, but at the same time players sense not only that it's scary, but there's a sense of sadness."
- **Elden Ring - Godrick the Grafted**: Embodies "the feeling of sadness and frustration when a Lord comes to the end of his reign, trying to cling desperately to the power he still has left."
- **Dark Souls - Artorias of the Abyss**: A legendary knight the player has heard about throughout the game, now corrupted and tragic. The fight carries the weight of his story.
- **Sekiro - Isshin, the Sword Saint**: The final boss embodies the game's entire combat philosophy, testing everything the player has learned while telling a story of a warrior's undying will.

### Review Checkpoints

- Do bosses convey emotion beyond "threatening enemy"?
- Is there contradiction in the boss's character (strength and weakness, beauty and horror)?
- Does the boss design communicate lore about the world?
- Does the surrounding level design build anticipation for the boss encounter?
- Does defeating the boss produce complex emotions (triumph mixed with something else)?

## 9. Combat Philosophy: Weight, Evolution, and Commitment

### Principle

Combat should feel weighty and consequential, with every action carrying commitment. Different games can explore different points on the spectrum from deliberate to aggressive, but the core principle of meaningful commitment to actions remains constant.

### Explanation

Miyazaki's combat design has evolved across titles, each exploring a different philosophy while maintaining the principle that player actions must carry weight and consequence:

| Title | Philosophy | Core Mechanic | Pace |
|-------|-----------|--------------|------|
| Demon's Souls / Dark Souls | Deliberate, methodical, survival | Stamina management | Slow, weighty |
| Bloodborne | Aggressive, offensive | Rally (health regain on attack) | Fast, offensive |
| Sekiro | Rhythmic, precision | Posture / Deflection | Intense, reactive |
| Elden Ring | Flexible, open | Stance break + Spirit Ashes | Variable by build |

The stamina system in Souls games forces constant risk-reward decisions. Bloodborne's Rally system mechanically enforces aggression by letting players recover health through immediate counter-attacks. Sekiro's posture system creates a rhythm of sword clashing where deflection is as important as attacking.

> "Sekiro was a big turning point... Personally, I think there's one more level we can crank it up to and sharpen that and hone in on that mechanic even more."
> -- PC Gamer

Miyazaki explicitly "avoided rigid weapon-design rules to prevent conservative limitations" and conceived of skills as "mythological tools used by heroes against mythological threats, prioritizing authentic drama over pure balance."

### Application Examples

- **Dark Souls - Stamina System**: Every action (attack, dodge, block, sprint) consumes stamina, creating constant decisions about resource allocation in combat.
- **Bloodborne - Rally System**: "Represents the player's increased will to continue after successfully striking an enemy." Punishes passivity; rewards aggression.
- **Sekiro - Deflection**: "Since there's no need to balance various builds or tune the combat system to PvP, the timing of deflect is quite generous and, more importantly, a missed deflect seamlessly turns into a guard."
- **Elden Ring - Build Diversity**: Stance breaking deepens melee combat from Dark Souls, while Spirit Ashes and diverse weapon skills enable multiple approaches to every encounter.

### Review Checkpoints

- Do combat actions carry meaningful commitment (animation commitment, resource cost)?
- Is there a clear risk-reward dynamic in every combat decision?
- Does the combat system evolve or deepen as the player progresses?
- Are both offensive and defensive actions (inward and outward verbs) meaningful?
- Does the combat "feel" match the game's thematic identity?

## 10. Unified Difficulty (Shared Experience as Community)

### Principle

A single, unified difficulty level creates shared cultural experiences and community discussion. Fragmenting the player base through difficulty settings undermines the collective sense of accomplishment and the community that forms around shared challenges.

> "We don't want to include a difficulty selection because we want to bring everyone to the same level of discussion and the same level of enjoyment. We want everyone to feel that sense of accomplishment. We want everyone to feel elated and to join that discussion on the same level."
> -- GameSpot

> "We feel if there's different difficulties, that's going to segment and fragment the user base."
> -- GameSpot

### Explanation

Rather than adjusting difficulty through settings, Miyazaki provides accessibility through design: summoning other players, multiple viable build paths, optional areas for leveling, and (in Elden Ring) the freedom to leave and return to challenges. The difficulty is part of the game's identity, but accessibility is embedded in the systems themselves.

> "In Elden Ring, we have not intentionally tried to lower the game's difficulty, but I think more players will finish it this time."
> -- Kotaku

> "I do feel apologetic toward anyone who feels there's just too much to overcome in my games. I just want as many players as possible to experience the joy that comes from overcoming hardship."
> -- The New Yorker, 2022

### Application Examples

- **Dark Souls - Summoning**: Players can summon allies (NPCs or other players) to help with difficult encounters, providing organic difficulty adjustment without a settings menu.
- **Elden Ring - Open World**: Players who struggle with one area can explore elsewhere, gain power, and return. The open structure is itself an accessibility feature.
- **Dark Souls - Build Variety**: Sorcery, pyromancy, faith builds, and heavy armor/shield builds each offer different approaches to the same challenges, naturally accommodating different skill levels.
- **Sekiro - Limited Flexibility**: With no build variety, Sekiro is the most demanding title. This was a deliberate choice to focus the combat experience on a single, refined system.

### Review Checkpoints

- Does a unified difficulty create a shared community experience?
- Are there organic accessibility tools within the game's systems (summoning, exploration, builds)?
- Does the community form around shared challenges and discoveries?
- Is the difficulty integral to the game's identity and emotional arc?
- Are players who struggle given paths forward without undermining the core experience?

## 11. Abstract Creative Direction (Team Leadership)

### Principle

Creative direction should provide emotional and thematic guidance rather than specific technical instructions. Abstract direction preserves the creative freedom of team members while maintaining a unified vision. Over-specification produces work that is technically correct but creatively lifeless.

> "If your instructions are too specific, the designs you get will be somewhat devoid of creativity, so I try to give out just the most basic, essential information before handing it over to the artists' imaginations."
> -- Design Works Interview

### Explanation

Miyazaki's direction style gives designers emotional briefs rather than technical specifications. For equipment design, he would say "make something you can trust with your life on the battlefield" rather than specifying materials, shapes, or proportions. This approach produces richer results because each artist's unique interpretation adds a layer of authenticity. Miyazaki maintains final authority on all creative decisions (planning, art, story, music) through his "Unified Production Workflow," but within that structure, he maximizes individual creative freedom.

> "I decided to give designers as much freedom as possible while maintaining a certain level of solidarity in the game's art work, hoping that designers' unique quirks would somehow work together to produce a special kind of harmony."

### Application Examples

- **Dark Souls - Equipment Design**: "I really wanted players to say, 'I trust this with my life.' Whether it's a sword, shield or armor, I want it to evoke some kind of feeling."
- **Elden Ring - George R. R. Martin Collaboration**: Miyazaki gave Martin "very vague and broad themes and ideas for the mythos" rather than detailed specifications, specifically to avoid limiting Martin's creative output.
- **General Art Direction**: Miyazaki organized Dark Souls around three primary visual themes (divine imagery, chaos/demons, death imagery) as emotional anchors, not rigid style guides.

### Review Checkpoints

- Does creative direction provide emotional/thematic guidance rather than rigid specifications?
- Do team members have room to contribute their own creative interpretation?
- Is there a unified vision that individual contributions serve?
- Do abstract briefs produce work with more character than detailed specifications would?
- Is the final creative authority clearly defined while allowing freedom within that structure?

## 12. The Harsh World (Worldview as Design Foundation)

### Principle

The game world reflects a worldview where existence is harsh and unforgiving, but within that harshness lies the seed of growth and beauty. Being thrust into adversity and finding the will to persist is a universal human experience that games can honor.

> "I have always felt that the world was quite a harsh place. I've always lived my life with that worldview, those values. So this idea of being thrust into a wasteland and planting the seeds of growth is something that, for me, feels almost universal."
> -- The Guardian, 2024

### Explanation

This principle ties together Miyazaki's design philosophy into a coherent worldview. The world is hostile; the player is vulnerable; progress is hard-won but meaningful. This is not nihilism -- it is the belief that adversity gives meaning to triumph. The bonfires, the moments of cooperation with strangers, the rare beautiful vista amid the ruins -- these carry emotional weight precisely because the surrounding world is harsh.

### Application Examples

- **Dark Souls - Firelink Shrine**: A rare place of safety and warmth in a hostile world. Its emotional impact depends on the harshness of everything surrounding it.
- **Bloodborne - Hunter's Dream**: The dream serves as respite, and its eventual fate carries weight because of the nightmare the player has endured.
- **Elden Ring - Roundtable Hold**: A gathering place for Tarnished who have been rejected by Grace, finding community in shared adversity.

### Review Checkpoints

- Does the world feel genuinely harsh and challenging to exist in?
- Are moments of beauty, safety, and connection made more powerful by contrast?
- Does adversity give meaning to the player's progress?
- Is there hope within the darkness (not pure nihilism)?
- Does the worldview feel authentic rather than manufactured?

## Consolidated Design Review Checklist

A quick-reference checklist for reviewing game designs using Miyazaki's principles.

### Core Experience

- [ ] Overcoming challenges produces genuine accomplishment (not frustration)
- [ ] Difficulty is fair -- deaths are learnable, not arbitrary
- [ ] Death has meaning beyond failure (teaches, creates stakes, serves narrative)
- [ ] The game avoids "unreasonable" design (player can always understand what happened)

### Narrative and World

- [ ] Story is delivered through fragments, not exposition
- [ ] Item descriptions, environments, and NPC behavior carry lore
- [ ] Intentional gaps invite player interpretation and community discussion
- [ ] The world feels like a cohesive, interconnected whole
- [ ] Spatial design creates moments of revelation (shortcuts, unexpected connections)

### Discovery and Agency

- [ ] Genuine secrets and hidden content reward curiosity
- [ ] NPCs behave as world inhabitants, not player-serving dispensers
- [ ] Players can discover and enjoy things in unintended ways
- [ ] The unknown is preserved -- not everything is signposted

### Multiplayer and Community

- [ ] Unified difficulty creates shared experience and community
- [ ] Indirect cooperation systems create connection without breaking atmosphere
- [ ] Organic accessibility exists within game systems (summoning, exploration, builds)

### Aesthetics and Emotion

- [ ] Dark imagery conveys emotional depth (sorrow, dignity) not just shock
- [ ] Beauty and horror coexist and amplify each other
- [ ] Bosses embody contradiction (fearsome yet tragic, powerful yet pitiable)
- [ ] Moments of safety/beauty gain impact from surrounding harshness

### Combat and Mechanics

- [ ] Actions carry meaningful commitment (animation, resource cost)
- [ ] Risk-reward dynamic is present in every combat decision
- [ ] Both offensive and defensive actions are meaningful
- [ ] Combat feel matches the game's thematic identity

### Production

- [ ] Creative direction uses abstract/emotional briefs, not rigid specifications
- [ ] Team members have creative freedom within a unified vision
- [ ] The harsh-but-meaningful worldview is consistently expressed
